Project Description OncoMX Sources Workshop Objective
OncoMX is an integrated cancer genomics web portal, developed under community guidance, to facilitate better use of and access to widely distributed cancer mutation and expression data. OncoMX is a collaboration between the George Washington University (GW), NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), the Swiss Institute of Bioinformatics (SIB), and the University of Delaware (UD). The core knowledgebase of OncoMX is derived from BioMuta and BioXpress integrated cancer mutation and expression databases. Normal expression data from Bgee and custom text mining software augment the cancer data to improve functional interpretation of the reported variants and expression profiles. All data are wrapped into the OncoMX database and web portal, mapped to additional functional information from NCI Early Detection Research Network (EDRN) and Reactome. It is expected that the large-scale integration of cancer data and supporting information, provided by OncoMX with direct community feedback, will benefit cancer research by improving synthesis of information and may make earlier detection a reality. OncoMX retrieves and integrates information from a number of data sources:

The goal of this workshop is to facilitate an interactive discussion between developers, end-users, and other cancer research community members to identify major wants and needs with respect to functionality and usability features of OncoMX web portal development. This workshop will bring together key members of the cancer biomarker detection community, including PIs from other ITCR projects, PIs from the EDRN community, members of the biomedical ontology community, NCBI, NCI GDC, UniProt, cancer biomarker researchers, and clinicians. Collected feedback will actively guide both short-term developments and longer-term hardening efforts. Anticipated user wants/needs could be but are not limited to: integrating additional expression data, notably ICGC, GTEx, and Expression Atlas; updating alignment and quantification pipelines to the newer generation of alignment algorithms, and updating the differential expression workflow to iterative/combined approaches; and adding the ability to detect involvement of alternative splicing events in pathogenisis.
